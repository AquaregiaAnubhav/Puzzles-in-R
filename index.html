Expected Rolls for a 10‑Sided Die to Push the LCM Beyond 2000

Author

Anubhav Prakash

1.  REMARK : Only the faces 7, 8, 9 and (5 or 10) matter

We first show that exceeding 2000 can happen only when every one of the following prime‑power factors has appeared at least once among the rolled faces:

  • \(2^{3}\) - supplied by the face 8;
  • \(3^{2}\) - supplied by the face 9;
  • \(5\) - supplied by the face 5 or 10;
  • \(7\) - supplied by the face 7.

 1.1 The absolute upper‑bound LCM for faces 1 to 10

The largest prime‑factor exponents available on a single die face are

face prime factorisation
8 \(2^{3}\)
9 \(3^{2}\)
5, 10 \(5^{1}\)
7 \(7^{1}\)

Multiplying those highest exponents gives the maximal possible LCM

\[ \operatorname*{lcm}\{1,2,\dots,10\} \;=\; 2^{3}\,\cdot\,3^{2}\,\cdot\,5\,\cdot\,7 \;=\; 2520. \]

Because \(2520>2000\), any LCM that has not yet reached 2520 must still be below 2000.

 1.2 “Relevant” versus “irrelevant” faces

A running LCM fails to hit 2520 iff it is missing at least one of the four prime factors listed above. Faces not carrying those factors -\(\{1,2,3,4,6\}\)- can never introduce a new required factor, hence they have no influence over whether the stopping condition is met.

Consequently, the game’s state can be summarised by four Boolean flags: \(\text{Seen7},\text{Seen8},\text{Seen9},\text{Seen5/10}\). The moment all four flags flip to \(\text{TRUE}\), the cumulative LCM leaps to 2520 and the process stops.

 1.3 Reduction to a non‑uniform coupon‑collector process

We now label four “coupons” that must be collected:

coupon fulfilled when … probability per roll
A face 7 appears \(p_A = 0.1\)
B face 8 appears \(p_B = 0.1\)
C face 9 appears \(p_C = 0.1\)
D either 5 or 10 appears \(p_D = 0.2\)

Rolling continues until all four coupons have been seen. That is exactly a coupon‑collector problem with heterogeneous probabilities \((0.1,0.1,0.1,0.2)\).

2. Expected number of rolls via indicator variables & inclusion–exclusion

Let \(X\) be the total number of rolls required. Define the indicator variables

\[ Z_i \;=\; \begin{cases} 1, & \text{if after the first $i$ rolls we are *still missing* at least one coupon},\\[2pt] 0, & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases} \]

Because \(Z_i=1\) precisely when we have not yet stopped at time \(i\), the well‑ known identity

\[X \;=\; 1 + \sum_{i=1}^{\infty} Z_i\]

gives the expectation

\[ \mathbb E[X] \;=\; 1 + \sum_{i=1}^{\infty} q_i,\quad q_i := \Pr(Z_i=1). \]

Our task reduces to evaluating each \(q_i\).

2.1  Setting up the four “missing‑coupon” events

For a fixed prefix length \(i\) denote

  • \(A =\) “no 7 has appeared”; single‑roll survival probability \(9/10\).
  • \(B =\) “no 8 has appeared”; survival \(9/10\).
  • \(C =\) “no 9 has appeared”; survival \(9/10\).
  • \(D =\) “neither 5 nor 10 has appeared”; survival \(8/10\).

Then \(q_i = \Pr(A\cup B\cup C\cup D)\).

2.2  Applying the inclusion–exclusion principle

For four events the principle states

\[ \Pr(A\cup B\cup C\cup D) = S_1 - S_2 + S_3 - S_4, \] where \(S_j\) is the sum of all size‑\(j\) intersections.


term # of such intersections single‑roll survival prob. contribution to \(q_i\)
Singles \(S_1\) \(A,B,C\) (3) \((9/10)^i\) \(3(9/10)^i\)
\(D\) (1) \((8/10)^i\) \(+(8/10)^i\)
Pairs \(S_2\) \(AB,AC,BC\) (3) \((8/10)^i\) \(3(8/10)^i\)
\(AD,BD,CD\) (3) \((7/10)^i\) \(+3(7/10)^i\)
Triples \(S_3\) \(ABC\) (1) \((7/10)^i\) \((7/10)^i\)
\(ABD,ACD,BCD\) (3) \((6/10)^i\) \(+3(6/10)^i\)
Quadruple \(S_4\) \(ABCD\) (1) \((5/10)^i\) \((5/10)^i\)

Combining with alternating signs we obtain

\[ q_i = \bigl[3(9/10)^i + (8/10)^i\bigr] - \bigl[3(8/10)^i + 3(7/10)^i\bigr] + \bigl[(7/10)^i + 3(6/10)^i\bigr] - (5/10)^i. \]

Multiplying numerator and denominator by \(10^i\) to display integer bases gives

\[ q_i \;=\; \frac{3\,9^{i} - 2\,8^{i} - 2\,7^{i} + 3\,6^{i} - 5^{i}} {10^{i}}. \]

That is exactly the expression obtained by the inclusion–exclusion bookkeeping and matches the formula quoted in the informal explanation.

2.3  Summing the geometric series

Because each term in \(q_i\) is geometric, the infinite sum has a closed form. For a ratio \(r\) with \(|r|<1\) \[\sum_{i=1}^{\infty} r^{i}=\frac{r}{1-r}.\] Applying that to the five ratios \(9/10,\;8/10,\;7/10,\;6/10,\;5/10\) and plugging in the coefficients yields

\[ \begin{aligned} \mathbb E[X] &= 1 + 3\Bigl(\frac{9}{10}/\frac{1}{10}\Bigr) -2\Bigl(\frac{8}{10}/\frac{2}{10}\Bigr) -2\Bigl(\frac{7}{10}/\frac{3}{10}\Bigr)\\[4pt] &\quad +3\Bigl(\frac{6}{10}/\frac{4}{10}\Bigr) -\Bigl(\frac{5}{10}/\frac{5}{10}\Bigr)\\[6pt] &= 1 + 27 - 8 - 14 + 18 - 5\\[4pt] &= \boxed{\dfrac{113}{6} \;\approx\; 18.833}. \end{aligned} \]

Hence on average 18.83 rolls are required for the LCM to exceed 2000.
